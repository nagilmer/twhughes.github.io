About

As of summer of '17 I am starting my 4th year in the Stanford Applied Physics PhD program working under Professor Shanhui Fan. Broadly, I do research in computational and theoretical photonics. I am part of the Accelerator on a Chip International Program , which has the goal of building miniature particle accelerators on a chip using advanced laser and nanofabrication technology. My main research interest is in computational design with applications to photonic devices: I am developing numerical and theoretical tools to solve scientifically interesting optimization problems. In addition, I am thinking about efficient methods for supplying the optical power needed to drive these accelerators. Please see my ‘research’ tab for more details.

On the side, I am a student of AI, machine learning, and evolutionary computation, and looking for ways to apply the most current advances in these fields to photonics, whether in implementing novel algorithms for device design or designing photonics-based hardware platforms that can perform these computations more efficiently.

When I’m not doing science, you can find me surfing, rock climbing, or experimenting with various fermentation projects. See my projects page for more details.

I grew up in sunny San Diego and went to the University of Michigan for my undergraduate. While I was initially a Philosophy major, I defected halfway into my undergrad and graduated with a BS in physics. In addition to physics I spent a good amount of focus in studying complex systems. Of particular interest was social network theory, nonlinear dynamics, and emergent phenomena. I also did research with Prof. Stephen Forrest on making economical thin-film solar cells, which gave me a lot of experience working with dangerous chemicals in the cleanroom. After that, I spent some time traveling in Asia, doing cold ion research in Singapore, and working as a software developer at GudTech Inc. before starting my PhD.
